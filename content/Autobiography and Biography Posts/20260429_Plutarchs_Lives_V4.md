I’ve started reading the book **[Plutarch’s Lives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_Lives)** by the Greek historian and biographer, **Plutarch** (50-120 AD). This book is so big that it's broken down into multiple volumes and will probably take me many years to finish reading end-to-end. *Plutarch’s Lives* has influenced many consequential people throughout history, including many of America’s founding fathers. The book is a biographical collection of venerated Greeks and Romans, forming 23 pairs of lives that are then compared and contrasted. Most of the book is present and preserved from antiquity, but parts of the book is missing. I’ve become an amateur historian in the past few years because of the following reason: we can only know where we’re headed in the future by studying where we’ve been in the past. By developing virtue and character, we can all be better individually and that will translate into society being better collectively. These are my thoughts and notes as I work through *Plutarch’s Lives (Vol. 4) - A. H. Clough*. We’ll see how long it takes me to make it to the end!

**Alexander the Great**

> “My son," said she, "thou art invincible.” - Said to Alexander by the Oracle of Apollo at Delphi

*Alexander the Great* of Macedonia is one of the lives Plutarch writes extensively about. In fact, Plutarch’s writings about Alexander the Great is the first biographical profile we have of who Alexander is. Alexander is most famous for building one of the largest empires in ancient history that stretched from Greece all the way to India. Legend has it that Alexander tamed the horse *Bucephalus* at age ~12-13 when no other person could, proving his courage to his father Phillip II. Philip subsequently told Alexander: “*O my son, look thee out a kingdom equal to and worthy of thyself, for Macedonia is too little for thee*.” Alexander took power as King of Macedon at age 20 when his father was assassinated. Alexander had a world view shaped by his fantastic tutor Aristotle who taught him good character; he always had an inherent self-belief, drive, and total conviction in what he was doing. He was allergic to living comfortably and was never afraid of danger, viewing his glory as more important to him than comfort or worldly pleasures. In the span of four years, he defeated Darius of Persia and conquered most of the known world. Then he pushed further beyond Persia, into the far east, reaching as far as India. He died at age ~32  and left a legacy that shaped the world forever. What I found interesting in Plutarch's profile were the following character traits that we may perhaps learn something from:

* Alexander had unshakeable **self-belief**. He literally believed that he could do anything and that he was going to succeed in all that he did. Literally nothing fazed him, and that’s what made him an inspiring leader.
* Alexander was characterized by a strong **obstinacy**. When he decided on an objective, he stopped at nothing to achieve that objective. He was never afraid of any danger in battle and often led from the frontlines. He was often personally injured in battles and had to be resuscitated back to nominal health, enduring extreme pain.
* Alexander lived with **temperance** his whole life. He was not a big fan of food or the pleasures of life. Rather he partook in those things sparingly, almost viewing them like one would view a household chore. What did light his fire was glory and the possibility of making an impact on a grand scale.
* Alexander was **magnanimous** with his materials, wealth, and mercy. He often treated his soldiers well, as well as the people he defeated in battle mercifully. He was fierce in battle, but also just as gracious when he did win. He respected friends and foes with courage and guts, because that’s who he was and he saw himself in anyone with those traits.
