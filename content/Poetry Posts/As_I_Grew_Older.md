**As I Grew Older by Langston Hughes**

> It was a long time ago.  
> I have almost forgotten my dream.  
> But it was there then,  
> In front of me,  
> Bright like a sun—  
> My dream.  
> And then the wall rose,  
> Rose slowly,  
> Slowly,  
> Between me and my dream.  
> Rose until it touched the sky—  
> The wall.  
> Shadow.  
> I am black.  
> I lie down in the shadow.  
> No longer the light of my dream before me,  
> Above me.  
> Only the thick wall.  
> Only the shadow.  
> My hands!  
> My dark hands!  
> Break through the wall!  
> Find my dream!  
> Help me to shatter this darkness,  
> To smash this night,  
> To break this shadow  
> Into a thousand lights of sun,  
> Into a thousand whirling dreams  
> Of sun!  

<u>Commentary</u>: The poem *As I Grew Older* by the American poet **Langston Hughes** (1901 – 1967) is about as real as it gets. It’s both sobering and somewhat hopeful. From my experiences, the sentiments expressed in the poem are so true as one grows older and accumulates life experience. The poem is a metaphor for the human condition where we let society slowly but surely snuff out our dreams. We let things such as career expectations, family obligations, accumulating material goods, constant social comparisons, and other distractions slowly but surely take over; after years of fulfilling societal obligations, we become lost and forget who we are as people. It’s like a “wall” that “rose slowly, slowly…” until the dream is snuffed out. By that time, only dramatic action to “break through the wall” will there be a chance for the dream to be recovered. Only then does a person rediscover the “light” they once had in their youth. And that’s if they are lucky: The truth is that many people never actually “break through the wall” and they resign themselves to the situation they are in for perpetuity. Past a certain point, a person has no choice nor mobility to maneuver based on the decisions of the past.

Think how many people had dreams as kids where they wanted to be something (i.e. an astronaut, author, business owner, etc.), but ended up somewhere not even close to what they once wanted? I can’t speak for women, but for men there’s a saying by **Henry David Thoreau**: “*Most men live lives of quiet desperation*.” The saying is so true. Many men (especially men in mid-life) desperately want to be someone or do something that makes them feel alive, but can’t anymore because of obligations and the limited mobility that obligations present. So they go back everyday to a job with no passion, no drive, and no sense of ownership, with no idea how to change the situation.

Therefore I say to the young people: *Dare to dream greatly, be brave enough to go after your dreams, and protect your dreams at all costs*. *This is not selfish and don't apologize to anyone for this, ever*.
